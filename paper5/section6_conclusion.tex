\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

For centuries, the Separation of Powers was a legal ideal enforced
by social norms, constitutional friction, and the difficulty of
simultaneously occupying multiple institutional roles. In the age of
algorithmic governance---where a single operator can define the rules,
execute the decisions, and control the records of millions of
individuals---those frictions have dissolved. The response cannot
be more friction; it must be structure.

We have shown that the properties Montesquieu identified as necessary
for institutional legitimacy---independence of the three powers and
mutual checks between them---can be encoded as formal properties of
software architecture:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Legislative Independence} is enforced by the
  immutability of linear resource types, verifiable via binary
  hash attestation (Theorem~\ref{thm:leg}).
  \item \textbf{Judicial Independence} is enforced by public Merkle
  roots and ZK proof soundness, requiring no operator cooperation
  (Theorem~\ref{thm:jud}).
  \item \textbf{Executive Containment} is enforced by the
  linear type chain that makes decision-without-logging an
  uncompilable programme (Theorem~\ref{thm:exec}).
\end{itemize}

The Constitutional Completeness corollary
(Corollary~\ref{cor:completeness}) proves that these three conditions
compose into a system where no single principal holds capabilities
across more than one constitutional branch. This is, to our
knowledge, the first formal proof that Montesquieu's separation of
powers can be satisfied---not merely approximated---by a
computational architecture.

The implications reach beyond AI systems. Any institution that
operates at scale over digital state---a bank, a government registry,
a healthcare provider, a content moderation platform---can be
evaluated against the Constitutional Invariant
(Definition~\ref{def:invariant}). Those that satisfy it are
\emph{Verifiable Institutions}: their legitimacy is a mathematical
property, not a function of the operator's reputation.

Lessig wrote that ``code is law''~\cite{lessig1999}. Constitutional
Code is the converse: \emph{law is architecture}. The shift from
geocentric to heliocentric governance moves the centre of trust from
the operator to the protocol. The constitution is no longer a
document that constrains behaviour after the fact; it is a type
system that makes unconstitutional behaviour unrepresentable before
the first instruction executes.

The violation of the constitution is not a crime to be punished.
It is a compilation error that prevents the institution from
ever existing.
